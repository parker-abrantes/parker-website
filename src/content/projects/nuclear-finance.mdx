## Problem

SMR competitiveness is frequently evaluated against utility-scale solar on a levelized cost of energy (LCOE) basis, with nuclear consistently appearing uncompetitive. This framing misdiagnoses the problem.

**SMR competitiveness is not primarily a technology cost question — it is a financing structure question.**

Under the right combination of policy instruments, SMR LCOE can achieve parity with or advantage over utility-scale solar, particularly in markets requiring firm, dispatchable baseload capacity. Under the wrong combination, no improvement in reactor technology or construction efficiency closes the gap. Today, every developer, fund, and policy shop models this in bespoke Excel files. No standardized, assumption-adjustable, benchmarked platform exists.

## What I Built

An open-source Python framework (`nuclear-pf`) that computes LCOE, DSCR, and equity IRR across configurable financing structures:

- DOE Loan Programs Office guarantee scenarios
- Merchant financing
- Regulated utility rate base
- Hybrid structures with IRA production tax credit toggles

The framework includes real-project presets anchored to three case studies representing distinct financing architectures and outcomes:

| Project | Architecture | Outcome |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Vogtle Units 3 & 4 | Regulated utility rate base, no LPO guarantee | 8 years late, $20B+ over budget |
| NuScale / TVA CFPP | Merchant + PPA, no LPO guarantee | Cancelled 2023 |
| Constellation-Microsoft PPA (TMI restart) | Merchant + long-term corporate PPA | Closed — the working model |

## What I Learned

The same reactor at the same cost can be financially viable or financially impossible depending entirely on the financing structure around it. Policy is not a subsidy — it is load-bearing infrastructure for deployment.
