There is a particular kind of exhaustion that comes from a Saturday morning rush at Starbucks. Not the tired-from-studying kind. The kind where you have been on your feet for six hours, there are seventeen drinks on the bar, the drive-through timer is screaming, and two of your partners called out. You don't stop. You can't stop. You just move.

I have a 3.3 GPA in aerospace engineering. I have helped coordinate a 25-member rocket team through an 8-month development cycle. I have debugged ROS navigation stacks and modeled nuclear project finance from first principles.

And I will tell you honestly: managing a Starbucks peak hour is harder than most of it.

Not intellectually. Emotionally and operationally. The margin for error is zero. The feedback is immediate. The stakes are completely real to the person standing in front of you, even if the stakes feel trivial from the outside. You learn quickly that how you treat people when you are tired and overwhelmed is closer to your actual character than how you treat them when things are easy.

Engineering taught me how to think about systems. Starbucks taught me how to run one under pressure, with real people, in real time, when things go wrong.

I don't think those are separate lessons.
