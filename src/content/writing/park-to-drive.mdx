I grew up in Providence, Rhode Island. I graduated high school in North Attleboro, Massachusetts, in a town where most people I knew were not particularly interested in leaving, or at least not in the direction I wanted to go.

Florida Tech was not the obvious choice. It was not even a school I had heard of until I was looking for aerospace programs that were serious about the work, not just the name on the diploma. I found it. I applied. I got in. And then I had to actually go.

There is a specific feeling of driving away from a place you have known your whole life. You don't feel free at first. You feel the weight of everything you're leaving behind, and the absence of everything you're moving toward. The car is in park. You put it in drive. Three clicks forward.

That is most of it, actually. Most of becoming something is not the arrival. It is the three clicks. The decision to move before you feel ready, before you have proof it will work, before anyone has told you it's a good idea.

I did not have a roadmap for aerospace engineering. I didn't know what ROS was, or what a DSCR was, or how to build a rocket. I had curiosity and enough stubbornness to not stop when things got hard.

That is the thing about putting the car in drive: it doesn't mean you know where you're going. It just means you've decided to find out.
